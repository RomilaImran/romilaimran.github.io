Investigating Whole-scalp Accuracy-specific Neural Correlates Underlying Reinforcement Learning

This project delves into the intricate neural mechanisms that underpin reinforcement learning processes, specifically examining how accuracy-related signals manifest across the entire scalp. Using advanced neuroimaging techniques, we aim to identify and characterize the specific brain activities that correlate with successful and unsuccessful learning outcomes.

Methodology

Our approach combines sophisticated behavioral tasks with whole-scalp neuroimaging data acquisition. Participants engaged in a custom-designed reinforcement learning paradigm where their decisions yielded distinct accuracy feedback. Electroencephalography (EEG) data was recorded simultaneously to capture high temporal resolution neural activity.

Advanced signal processing and machine learning techniques were then applied to the raw EEG data to extract relevant neural correlates. Special attention was paid to components associated with feedback processing and decision-making.

Key Findings

Preliminary results indicate distinct patterns of neural activation following accurate versus inaccurate responses. We observed significant differences in power spectral densities and event-related potentials (ERPs) across specific scalp regions, particularly in areas associated with cognitive control and reward processing.

These findings suggest that the brain not only registers the outcome of a learning trial but also generates specific, distributed neural signals reflecting the accuracy of the decision, which are crucial for subsequent behavioral adjustments.

Conclusion and Future Work

This investigation provides novel insights into the neural underpinnings of reinforcement learning, highlighting the whole-scalp dynamics of accuracy-specific neural correlates. Future work will focus on refining these correlates and exploring their causal roles using brain stimulation techniques.
